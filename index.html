What if we naively trained a policy on the human-aligned unsegmented data? ☹️

In our ablation study, we found that the policy trained on human-aligned data failed to complete any tasks, despite often moving in sensible directions. This failure is likely due to a distribution shift in the observation space between training and deployment.

Interestingly, the policy trained on segmented data performed well even when the robot was unsegmented at inference. We hypothesize that removing the human helped the model avoid overfitting to strong correlations between human and gripper actions, allowing it to focus on taking the learning signal from the task-relevant parts of the scene.

Abstract

Recent advances in imitation learning have shown great promise for developing robust robot manipulation policies using collected demonstrations. However, this promise is contingent on the availability of diverse, high-quality demonstration datasets, which remain challenging and costly to gather. Portable handheld grippers have recently emerged as intuitive and scalable alternatives to traditional robotic teleoperation methods for data collection, but their reliance solely on wrist-mounted cameras poses limitations in capturing large scene contexts. In this paper, we introduce MV-UMI (Multi-View Universal Manipulation Interface), a framework that integrates a wrist-mounted camera with a third-person perspective to overcome these limitations. This integration not only mitigates domain shifts between human demonstration and robot deployment but also preserves the cross-embodiment advantages of handheld data-collection devices. Additionally, we present a new modular three-jawed handheld gripper weighing 0.39 kg, which offers enhanced load capacity and dexterity, and is capable of lifting objects up to three times its own weight. Experimental results demonstrate that our framework improves performance in sub-tasks requiring broad scene understanding by approximately 47%, confirming the effectiveness of our approach in expanding the range of feasible manipulation tasks that can be demonstrated and learned using handheld gripper systems.

Mechanical Design

The design of MV-UMI is intended to be modular. Mounts are designed for the gripper, allowing it to be used handheld or when attached to a robot.
